## Summary

Yeast is the microorganism that converts wort sugars into alcohol and CO₂, but its chemical by-products — esters, phenols, higher alcohols, and organic acids — are equally important to final beer flavour. Yeast strain selection and fermentation temperature are the brewer's primary tools for controlling these flavour-active compounds. The distinction between top-fermenting ale yeasts and bottom-fermenting lager yeasts is the foundational split in beer classification.

## Explanation

### The Fermentation Equation (Simplified)

```
C₆H₁₂O₆  →  2 C₂H₅OH  +  2 CO₂
  Glucose       Ethanol      Carbon dioxide
```

In reality, yeast produces dozens of by-products alongside ethanol and CO₂ — and these minor compounds define much of the beer's character.

### Yeast Species

**Ale yeast** (*Saccharomyces cerevisiae*)
- Ferments at 15–25°C
- Rises to wort surface during active fermentation (top-fermenting)
- Produces significant esters (fruity) and phenols (spicy) depending on strain
- Flocculates (clumps and falls) at end of fermentation

**Lager yeast** (*Saccharomyces pastorianus*)
- Hybrid of *S. cerevisiae* × *S. eubayanus* (cold-tolerant wild yeast)
- Ferments at 4–12°C
- Settles at bottom (bottom-fermenting)
- Produces minimal esters and phenols → clean, crisp character

**Champagne yeast** (*Saccharomyces cerevisiae* var. *bayanus*)
- High alcohol tolerance; very high attenuation (ferments almost completely dry)
- Used in Viking Bubble Sour as secondary yeast — ferments residual sugars after primary ale fermentation

### Key Flavour-Active By-Products

**Esters** (fruity aromas)
- Formed by reaction of alcohols + organic acids during fermentation
- Production increases with: higher temperature, higher gravity wort, underpitching (too little yeast)
- Key esters:
  - *Isoamyl acetate*: banana (hallmark of Hefeweizen yeast strains)
  - *Ethyl acetate*: solvent-like at high concentrations; fruity at low levels
  - *Ethyl hexanoate*: apple, anise
  - *Isobutyl acetate*: banana, strawberry

**Phenols** (spicy, clove-like aromas)
- Produced by yeast strains with the **POF+ gene** (phenolic off-flavour positive — a feature, not a flaw, in certain ale yeasts)
- Key phenols:
  - *4-Vinyl guaiacol (4VG)*: clove — the defining character of Hefeweizen and some Belgian yeasts
  - *Guaiacol*: smoky, medicinal (often undesirable)
  - *4-Ethyl phenol*: leathery, barnyard (Brettanomyces character)

**Higher Alcohols (Fusel Alcohols)**
- Produced when yeast metabolises amino acids or when FAN (Free Amino Nitrogen) is low
- Warm fermentation and underpitching increase fusel production
- Noticeable as a harsh, warming sensation; "hot" beer
- Dissipate with conditioning and time

**Diacetyl** (buttery, butterscotch)
- Intermediate by-product secreted during fermentation; yeast reabsorbs it at end
- If yeast is removed before reabsorption (crash cooling too early), diacetyl remains
- Mitigation: **diacetyl rest** — briefly raise temperature before cold crash (explicit in Viking Low Lager recipe: 2 days at +15°C)
- Acceptable in small amounts in some styles (English ales, Czech Pilsner); off-flavour in most others

**Acetaldehyde** (green apple, cidery)
- Also a fermentation intermediate; should be reabsorbed by healthy, well-conditioned yeast
- Indicates young, rushed, or stressed fermentation

### Attenuation

**Attenuation** = % of wort sugars fermented by yeast.

`Apparent Attenuation (%) = ((OG − FG) / (OG − 1.000)) × 100`

**Real Attenuation** corrects for alcohol's effect on density.

| Attenuation Level | Meaning | Beer Character |
|------------------|---------|----------------|
| Low (<65%) | Many unfermentable dextrins remain | Full-bodied, sweet, less dry |
| Medium (65–75%) | Balanced | Most lagers and ales |
| High (>75%) | Very fermentable wort | Dry, crisp, thin-bodied |

**Controlling attenuation**:
- Mash at lower temperatures (62–64°C) → more fermentable (drier beer)
- Mash at higher temperatures (68–72°C) → less fermentable (fuller body)
- Adding Dextrin Malt → unfermentable dextrins → fuller body regardless of mash temp

### Pitching Rate

**Pitching rate** = amount of yeast added to wort (cells/mL/°Plato).

- Underpitching: yeast stressed → more esters + fusel alcohols + fermentation lag
- Overpitching: yeast clean but may lack flavour complexity; poor head retention
- Target: ~0.75–1M cells/mL/°P for ales; ~1.5M cells/mL/°P for lagers

### Fermentation Profiles from Viking Recipes

**Viking APA (Ale)**
- Yeast: Safale US-05
- 14 days at 19°C primary → 2 days at 5°C cold crash
- Result: Clean American ale character with hop aroma

**Viking Low Lager (Lager)**
- Yeast: Lallemand Diamond
- 5 days 13°C → 2 days 15°C (diacetyl rest) → 5 days 5°C → 2 days -1°C
- Gradual temperature stepping controls flavour development

**Viking Bubble Sour (Dual Yeast)**
- Primary: Ale yeast → alcohol + CO₂
- Secondary: Champagne yeast → complete fermentation to dryness
- Sourness from Maltax Sour extract (not yeast-derived lactic acid)

## Contradictions

> **Diacetyl as flaw vs feature**: Most brewing quality control treats diacetyl as an off-flavour to eliminate. However, small amounts of diacetyl are traditional and acceptable in Czech Pilsner and some English ales. The Viking handbook's explicit diacetyl rest in the Low Lager recipe targets complete elimination, consistent with clean lager style goals. Craft brewers targeting Czech-style Pilsner may intentionally retain a trace.

> **Esters as flaw vs feature**: In Hefeweizen and Belgian ales, esters are the defining flavour. In American lagers and clean ales (US-05), esters are minimised. The same by-product is simultaneously the goal and the enemy depending on style. Yeast strain selection and fermentation temperature together control whether ester production is encouraged or suppressed.
