## Summary

Dark malts is a general category covering specialty malts roasted at high temperatures (150–220°C+) to develop intense colour (200–1400+ EBC) and roasted flavours ranging from chocolate and coffee to char and tar. They have zero diastatic power and are used in small quantities (1–15%) to define the character of dark beer styles.

## Explanation

### The Dark Malt Spectrum

| Malt Type | EBC Range | Flavour | Primary Use |
|-----------|----------|---------|-------------|
| **Amber / Brown Malt** | 50–200 | Biscuit, nut, bread crust | Brown ales, porters |
| [[caramel-malt]] (dark) | 150–250 | Raisin, prune, molasses, dark fruit | Dark ales, stouts |
| [[chocolate-malt]] | 700–1000 | Chocolate, coffee, bittersweet | Porter, stout, brown ale |
| [[roasted-malts|Roasted Barley]] | 900–1300 | Dry bitter coffee, astringent | Dry stout, Irish Stout |
| [[black-malt]] | 1200–1500 | Espresso, char, very dry | Stouts, dark lager colouring |
| Carafa Special III | 1200–1500 | Coffee, chocolate — **no husk** | Smooth dark lager, Schwarzbier |

### Key Properties of Dark Malts

- **Zero diastatic power** — cannot convert starches; use only with base malt
- **Disproportionate colour impact** — even 1–3% dark malt dramatically shifts beer colour
- **Bitterness** — dark malts contribute "roast bitterness" from pyrolysis products (distinct from hop bitterness)
- **Antioxidant compounds** — melanoidins from Maillard reactions act as antioxidants, improving shelf life

### The Husk Issue

High-roast malts (roasted barley, black malt) include the husk, which contains tannins. At extreme kilning temperatures, husk tannins become harsh and astringent — contributing to the "dry" character of Irish Stout but creating off-flavours in styles where smoothness is desired.

**Solution**: Dehusked variants (e.g., Carafa Special by Weyermann; Viking's equivalents) remove the husk before roasting. This achieves the same dark colour without harsh tannins. Used in Schwarzbier (German black lager) where smooth dark character is essential.

### Flavour Chemistry

Dark malt flavour comes from two processes:
1. **Intense Maillard reaction** (>150°C): melanoidins, hundreds of aromatics, chocolate/coffee compounds
2. **Pyrolysis** (>180°C): thermal decomposition produces tar-like, charred, and bitter compounds

The balance of these reactions — controlled by temperature, time, and moisture — determines whether the result tastes like "milk chocolate" or "burnt espresso."

### Usage Guidelines

| Style Goal | Dark Malt Approach |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Stout / porter | 5–10% chocolate malt + 3–5% roasted barley |
| Subtle coffee hint | 2–3% chocolate malt |
| Sharp colour boost (no flavour) | 1% black malt |
| Smooth dark lager (Schwarzbier) | 3–5% dehusked Carafa Special |
| Dark red amber colour | 5–10% dark caramel malt |
