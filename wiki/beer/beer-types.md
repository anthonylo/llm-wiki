## Summary

Beers are primarily classified by fermentation type — **top fermented** (ales, using yeast that rise to the surface at warm temperatures) and **bottom fermented** (lagers, using yeast that sink and work at cold temperatures). Within each category, dozens of distinct styles exist, differentiated by malt selection, hop character, yeast strain, and alcohol content.

## Explanation

### Primary Classification: Fermentation Type

| Type | Yeast Behaviour | Temperature | Character |
|------|----------------|-------------|-----------|
| **Top fermented** (ales) | Yeast rises to surface | Warm (15–25°C) | Fruity esters, complex aromas |
| **Bottom fermented** (lagers) | Yeast sinks to bottom | Cold (4–12°C) | Clean, crisp, fewer esters |

See [[fermentation-types]] for the biochemistry.

### Bottom Fermented Beer Styles

**Pilsner**
- Origin: Bohemia, city of Pilsen
- Characteristics: golden, clear, fine hop aroma, moderate bitterness, smooth malty taste
- Water chemistry: very soft water essential
- Malts: predominantly pale [[malt-types|Pilsner malt]], optional CaraBody for body
- ABV: ~4.5–5.5%

**Lager**
- Most consumed beer type globally
- Enabled by refrigeration (late 19th century)
- Clean, carbonated, neutral character
- Wide range from light lager to strong exports
- Malts: Pilsner malt base ± Dextrin for body

**Märzen (Oktoberfest / Vienna Lager)**
- Origin: Germany; brewed in March (März), stored cold, consumed at autumn festivals
- Amber colour (7–15 EBC), moderate bitterness
- Malts: Vienna and Munich malts for amber colour and malt character
- ABV: ~5.0–6.0%

**Bock**
- Origin: Einbeck, Germany (14th century) — name means "buck" (male goat)
- Strong dark lager; seasonal (winter/spring)
- Rich malt character, low bitterness
- Variants: Maibock (pale), Doppelbock (very strong, 7–12%), Eisbock
- Malts: Munich, caramel, and dark malts

**Dark Lager**
- Pre-dates pale lagers; historically the dominant European style
- Central European origins (Bavaria, Bohemia)
- Mild roasted character, smooth
- Malts: Munich dark, caramel, small additions of roasted malts

### Top Fermented Beer Styles

**Ales**
- Umbrella category: includes Pale Ale, Bitter, Mild, IPA, Barleywine
- British Isles origin; now global
- Fruity ester character from warm fermentation
- Wide ABV range (3.5–12%)

Key sub-styles:
- *American Pale Ale (APA)*: lighter, citrusy hop character; easy-drinking
- *India Pale Ale (IPA)*: high hop bitterness and aroma; originally brewed strong for preservation
- *American IPA*: "new wave" hop varieties (citrus, tropical, floral); very popular in craft brewing
- *New England IPA (NEIPA)*: hazy, juicy, low bitterness; oats in grist for mouthfeel
- *Belgian IPA*: IPA character + Belgian yeast esters (spicy, fruity, high ABV)
- *Belgian Dubbel*: rich, malty, copper, complex yeast aromas
- *Belgian Tripel*: strong (8–10%), pale, highly carbonated, phenolic yeast

**Stouts and Porters**
- Origin: London; most strongly associated with Ireland
- Defined by roasted malt character (coffee, chocolate, molasses)
- Porters were historically more common; stouts eventually replaced them
- Baltic Porter: strong lager-fermented version (hybrid)
- Russian Imperial Stout: very strong (9–14%), rich, complex

**Wheat Beers**
- At least 50% wheat malt in the grist
- German Hefeweizen: yeast-turbid, banana (isoamyl acetate) and clove (4-vinyl guaiacol) aroma from specific yeast strain
- Belgian witbier: spiced with orange peel and coriander
- American Wheat: clean fermentation, hop-forward; no hefeweizen phenolics
- Refreshing, high carbonation, typically unfiltered

### Special / Niche Styles

**Grodziskie / Grätzer**: Polish historical style; 100% oak-smoked wheat malt; highly carbonated pale ale
**Smoked Lager**: pale lager with pear wood smoke character
**Sour Beer**: lactic acid fermentation (e.g., Viking Bubble Sour using Maltax Sour extract)
**Gluten-Free Lager**: brewed from malt extracts without barley gluten

### Key Brewing Parameters

| Parameter | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **OG** (Original Gravity) | Wort sugar density before fermentation; predicts final ABV |
| **FG** (Final Gravity) | Residual sugar after fermentation |
| **ABV** | (OG − FG) × 0.131 ≈ alcohol by volume |
| **EBC** | European Brewing Convention colour unit; lower = paler |
| **BU** (Bitterness Units) | IBU equivalent; measures hop bitterness |
| **Attenuation** | % of sugars fermented; higher = drier beer |

## Contradictions

> **No contradictions with AI papers**: This handbook covers brewing science; the other papers cover AI, context theory, and AGI. The two domains are orthogonal.

> **"Lager" as generic term**: The handbook notes that "lager" is sometimes incorrectly used to refer to all bottom-fermented beers. The specific Lager style is a subset of bottom-fermented beers, which also includes Pilsner, Märzen, Bock, and Dark Lager as distinct styles.
