## Summary

Bock is a strong lager beer originating from Germany with a long brewing history dating back to the 14th century. Characterized by its strong, copper-brown color, malty taste, and high alcohol content, bock has evolved into several distinctive styles including Dobbel Bock, Eisbock, and Maibock.

## Explanation

### Background

Bock is a strong lager beer with origins in German brewing traditions dating back to the 14th century. It has a well-established heritage in German beer culture and represents one of the strongest traditional lager styles.

### Characteristics

The original bock beer style is strong, copper-brown colored, moderately hopped beer with a malty taste. Several different styles have evolved over time:

- **Dobbel Bock**: A stronger version of traditional bock
- **Eisbock**: An even stronger version of dobbel bock
- **Maibock**: Lower in color with slightly more hops compared to the original style
- **Wheat Bock**: Similar styles but with 50%+ wheat malt in the malt bill

### Typical Analysis (Pale Bock)

- **Original gravity**: 16-18 °Plato
- **Alcohol content**: 6-8% volume
- **Color**: 8-15 °EBC (pale version)
- **Bitterness**: 15-25 BU

### Malt Composition

Many types of malt can be found in bock beer recipes due to bock's very malty nature:

- **Pale Bocks**: Pilsner Malt combined with low-color caramel malts and dark malts like Caramel Pale Malt and Munich Light & Dark Malts
- **Dark Versions**: About half of the grist can be formulated from Cookie Malt and Caramel Malt 100. For very deep color, a few percent of Chocolate Light Malt, Chocolate Dark Malt, or Black Malt may be used
- **Wheat Bock**: Requires Wheat Malt

### Brewing Notes

No particular brewing precautions are required for bock production.
