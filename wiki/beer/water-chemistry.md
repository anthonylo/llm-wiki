## Summary

Water is the largest ingredient in beer by volume and has profound effects on mash enzyme activity, hop bitterness perception, yeast health, and final beer character. The mineral content of brewing water historically defined regional beer styles — Pilsen's extremely soft water enabled the delicate hop character of Pilsner; Burton-on-Trent's sulphate-rich water accentuated the hop bitterness of pale ales. Modern brewers adjust water chemistry to match any target style.

## Explanation

### Why Water Chemistry Matters

Water minerals affect brewing in four ways:
1. **Mash pH** — critical for optimal enzyme activity (target 5.2–5.4 for most styles)
2. **Flavour** — sulphates enhance hop dryness; chloride enhances malt roundness
3. **Yeast health** — minerals like zinc and magnesium are yeast nutrients
4. **Hop bitterness perception** — sulphate amplifies perceived hop sharpness

### Key Minerals

| Ion | Impact | Typical Range |
|-----|--------|---------------|
| **Calcium (Ca²⁺)** | Lowers mash pH; promotes enzyme activity; yeast flocculation; clarity | 50–150 mg/L |
| **Magnesium (Mg²⁺)** | Yeast nutrient; sour/bitter at high levels | 10–30 mg/L |
| **Sodium (Na⁺)** | Enhances roundness and fullness at low levels; salty above ~150 mg/L | <150 mg/L |
| **Sulphate (SO₄²⁻)** | Dry, crisp, emphasises hop bitterness ("Burton snap") | 50–400 mg/L |
| **Chloride (Cl⁻)** | Smooth, full, enhances malt character | 50–200 mg/L |
| **Bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻)** | Raises mash pH; problematic for pale ales; beneficial for dark beers | varies |

### The Sulphate:Chloride Ratio

The ratio of sulphate to chloride is the practical guide for a beer's flavour direction:
- **High sulphate / low chloride** (>2:1): hoppy, dry, bitter → English IPA, American IPA
- **Balanced** (~1:1): neutral, versatile → many lager styles
- **Low sulphate / high chloride** (<0.5:1): malty, soft, full → Munich Helles, NEIPA

### Historical Water Profiles and Beer Styles

| City | Hardness | Key Mineral | Associated Style |
|------|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Pilsen (Czech Republic)** | Very soft (~50 mg/L total) | Low everything | Pilsner (delicate, soft bitterness) |
| **Burton-on-Trent (England)** | Very hard | High sulphate (~700 mg/L) | English Pale Ale, IPA |
| **Munich (Germany)** | Moderately hard | Bicarbonate | Munich Dunkel, Märzen |
| **Dublin (Ireland)** | Moderately hard | Bicarbonate | Stout (bicarbonate buffers roasted malt acidity) |
| **Dortmund (Germany)** | Balanced hard | Balanced | Export lager |

### Mash pH and the Viking Handbook

The Viking handbook repeatedly notes pH management:
- Viking Low Lager: "pH settles itself in the range of 5.3–5.5" — indicating their malt bill naturally hits the target
- Citric Wheat Beer: "pH adjustments done with citric acid"
- Dark Red Lager / extract recipes: "pH set with lactic acid to 5.0"

**Target mash pH: 5.2–5.4**
- Below this: enzyme activity reduces; beer may taste harsh
- Above this: beta-amylase becomes less active; fermentability drops; hop bitterness perception changes

### Adjusting Water Chemistry

Brewers modify water by:
- **Adding minerals**: gypsum (CaSO₄) adds calcium and sulphate; calcium chloride (CaCl₂) adds calcium and chloride; Epsom salt (MgSO₄) adds magnesium and sulphate
- **Diluting**: blend with RO (reverse osmosis) water to reduce hardness
- **Acidifying**: lactic acid (most common, clean flavour) or citric acid (as in Viking recipes) or acidulated malt to lower mash pH
- **Softening**: ion exchange or carbon filtration

### Water Hardness and Dark Beers

Dark malts are naturally acidic (roasted malts lower wort pH). High-bicarbonate water buffers this, keeping pH in an acceptable range for dark beer production. This is why historically dark beers came from hard-water cities (Munich, Dublin) — their water chemistry suited the style.

For pale beers in hard-water regions: bicarbonate must be removed or neutralised, otherwise mash pH rises too high, reducing enzyme efficiency and creating harsh flavours.

## Contradictions

> **Pilsner water chemistry**: The handbook notes Pilsner is "well known for its very soft brewing water, which contributes to its smooth taste." This soft water — extremely low in minerals — is essential for Pilsner's delicate hop character. Attempting to brew Pilsner-style beer with hard water without treatment will produce noticeably different (often harsher) results. This is why geographic origin historically mattered for style identity.

> **"Natural" pH vs adjustment**: The Viking Low Lager recipe notes pH settles naturally at 5.3–5.5 without adjustment. Other recipes require lactic or citric acid addition. The difference depends on malt bill (darker malts are more acidic), water mineral content, and mash thickness. There is no single universal approach — each recipe and water source combination requires individual assessment.
