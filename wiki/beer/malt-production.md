## Summary

Malting converts raw barley (or wheat, rye, oats) into brewing malt by controlled germination followed by kilning (drying). This process develops the enzymes and soluble sugars that brewers need to convert grain starch into fermentable sugars during mashing. Different kilning and roasting conditions produce the wide spectrum of [[malt-types]] — from pale Pilsner malt to pitch-black roasted barley.

## Explanation

### Why Malt?

Raw barley contains starch locked inside the endosperm behind protein and cell wall structures. To make beer, brewers need:
1. **Enzymes** (particularly amylases) to break starch into fermentable sugars
2. **Soluble starch** accessible to those enzymes
3. **Flavour precursors** developed through Maillard reactions during kilning

Malting achieves all three by mimicking natural germination and stopping it at the right moment.

### The Malting Process

**Stage 1: Steeping**
- Barley is soaked in water to raise moisture content from ~12% to ~42–46%
- Multiple wet/air rest cycles over 40–60 hours
- Activates the embryo and initiates germination
- Key: avoid over-steeping (creates sticky, damaged grain)
- Wheat: no husk → faster water uptake; great care needed to avoid over-steeping

**Stage 2: Germination**
- Grain germinates in controlled beds at 12–20°C for 4–6 days
- The embryo's gibberellins activate enzyme production in the aleurone layer
- **Endosperm modification**: cell walls and protein matrix are broken down, releasing starch
- Well-modified malt: starch fully accessible, high extract yield
- Indicators of modification: acrospire length (should reach 75–100% of grain length)

**Stage 3: Kilning**
- Germination halted by drying: temperature raised progressively from ~50°C to final kilning temperature
- Removes moisture from ~42% → ~4–5%
- Final temperature determines malt colour and flavour:

| Final Temp | Malt Type | Colour (EBC) |
|------------|-----------|--------------|
| 80–85°C | Pilsner malt | 3–4 |
| 90°C | Pale Ale malt | 4–7 |
| 100–105°C | Vienna malt | 7–10 |
| 105–110°C | Munich malt | 15–20 |

- Higher final temperatures → darker colour, more Maillard flavour compounds, lower enzyme activity

**Stage 4: Roasting** (for specialty malts)
- Caramel and dark malts require additional processing beyond standard kilning
- See [[malt-types]] for specific production of caramel and roasted malts

### Caramel Malt Production

Caramel malts use **green malt** (undried germinated grain) as input:
1. Green malt loaded into roasting drum with high moisture (>45%)
2. Drum sealed; high temperature causes internal starch to liquefy and caramelise
3. Once endosperm modification achieved, air flaps opened to remove moisture
4. Dried to <8% moisture; Maillard and caramelisation reactions develop colour and flavour
5. Result: glassy, caramelised endosperm with sweet, toffee/caramel flavours; no fermentable starch

Can also be produced in a specially equipped kiln rather than a drum.

### Dark/Roasted Malt Production

Two raw material paths:
1. **Dry pale brewing malt** roasted at high temperatures
2. **Native barley or cereals** roasted directly (roasted barley)

Processing:
- Temperatures exceed 200°C in roasting drum
- Very low moisture; Maillard reactions driven to completion
- Result: dark products with coffee, chocolate, liquorice, bitter notes
- No enzyme activity remains

### Wheat Malt Specifics

- Total malting time shorter than barley (no husk → faster steeping)
- Pale wheat malts: low kilning temperatures (~82°C max) to preserve light colour and flavour
- Higher protein content than barley → important for foam retention
- Used at 50%+ of grist for wheat beers; 2–5% as foam enhancer in other styles

### Key Malt Quality Parameters

| Parameter | Definition | Importance |
|-----------|------------|------------|
| Moisture | % water content | Affects storage stability; typically 4–5% |
| Extract (% d.m.) | Soluble material yield | Higher = more fermentable sugars per kg |
| Colour (EBC) | European Brewing Convention units | Beer colour prediction |
| Diastatic Power (WK) | Enzyme activity (Windisch-Kolbach) | Ability to convert starch; critical for high-adjunct recipes |
| Protein (% d.m.) | Total protein content | Affects head retention, haze, fermentation nutrition |
| FAN (mg/L) | Free Amino Nitrogen | Yeast nutrition; affects fermentation health |
| Viscosity (mPas) | Wort viscosity | High = filtration problems; related to beta-glucan content |
| pH | Wort acidity | Influences enzyme activity and beer stability |

See [[malt-characteristics]] for typical values across malt types.

## Contradictions

> **Caramel malt production methods**: The handbook mentions caramel malts can be produced in either a roasting drum or a specially equipped kiln, with different temperature profiles possible. The two methods produce somewhat different flavour profiles, but the handbook treats them as equivalent outputs. Some industry sources note drum-produced caramel malts have higher maximum temperatures and therefore more intense caramelisation.
