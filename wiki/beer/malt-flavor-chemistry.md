## Summary

The flavour diversity of malts — from light biscuity base malts to intensely bitter roasted barley — arises from two primary chemical reactions during kilning and roasting: the **Maillard reaction** (amino acids + reducing sugars at high heat) and **caramelisation** (sugar degradation at high temperature). The interplay of temperature, moisture, and time during malt production determines which compounds form, creating a vast flavour spectrum from honey and toffee to coffee and chocolate.

## Explanation

### The Two Core Reactions

**Maillard Reaction**
- Occurs when amino acids (from proteins) react with reducing sugars (glucose, maltose) at elevated temperatures
- Requires: temperature >140°C, low moisture
- Products: melanoidins (brown colour compounds), hundreds of aromatic compounds
- Flavours: bread crust, biscuit, toffee, nut, coffee, chocolate (depending on temperature and degree)
- Also produces colour (contributes to EBC)

**Caramelisation**
- Thermal degradation of sugars at high temperature (>170°C), without amino acids
- Produces caramel, toffee, butterscotch, burnt sugar aromas
- Key in caramel malt production (stewing green malt before drying)
- Distinct from Maillard — same colour results but different flavour compounds

Both reactions are **temperature-, moisture-, and time-dependent**:
- More time or higher temperature → more intense, darker flavours
- Moisture content modulates reaction rates — wet environments favour Maillard; dry environments favour pyrolysis (roasting)

### Flavour Formation by Temperature Stage

| Kilning/Roasting Temp | Moisture State | Reactions | Resulting Flavours |
|----------------------|----------------|-----------|-------------------|
| 50–65°C (drying) | High moisture → drying out | Enzymatic only; minimal Maillard | Clean, light malt |
| 80–90°C (pale kilning) | Low moisture | Light Maillard begins | Fresh bread, light biscuit (Pilsner, Pale Ale) |
| 100–110°C (Vienna/Munich) | Very low moisture | Moderate Maillard | Honey, malt, Vienna biscuit, light amber |
| 150–180°C (amber/brown) | Dry | Strong Maillard | Bread crust, nut, biscuit, toffee |
| 180–220°C (roasting) | Very dry | Intense Maillard + pyrolysis | Coffee, dark chocolate, molasses, bitter |
| >200°C (high roast) | Very dry | Pyrolysis dominant | Dark chocolate, espresso, char, tar |

**Green malt stewing (caramel malts)**: High moisture + elevated temperature → sugar caramelisation inside sealed endosperm → glassy, caramelised sugars (non-fermentable) with toffee, raisin, plum flavours.

### Flavour Wheel Description (Viking Handbook)

The handbook describes evaluating malts organoleptically across flavour profiles. Key descriptors:

**Base Malts (Pale, Pilsner)**
- Light, neutral, fresh, clean, slightly grainy
- Minimal roast or caramel character
- Background flavour enabling hops and yeast to express

**Vienna/Munich Malts**
- Bready, honey, light biscuit
- Warm malty character
- Define Märzen/Vienna lager style

**Caramel Malts (Light ~10 EBC)**
- Honey, light caramel, slightly sweet

**Caramel Malts (Medium ~50 EBC)**
- Toffee, rich caramel, amber-red colour

**Caramel Malts (Dark ~200 EBC)**
- Dark fruit (raisin, plum), prune, molasses

**Chocolate Malt (~800 EBC)**
- Milk chocolate, bittersweet chocolate, coffee

**Roasted Barley / Black Malt (~1100–1400 EBC)**
- Dark espresso, bitter chocolate, dry, astringent

### Malt Aroma Variations by EBC (from Viking Handbook Figure 8)

The handbook shows that as malt colour (EBC) increases across dark brewing malts:
- Light malts: malty, bready
- Medium malts: caramel, roasty
- Dark malts: coffee, chocolate, burnt
- Sensory profile changes are non-linear — small EBC additions at high colour ranges have large flavour impact

### Why Small Amounts of Dark Malt Matter

Even 2–5% of a high-EBC malt dramatically shifts beer character:
- 3% Roasted Barley (1200 EBC) in an otherwise pale recipe → stout colour
- 5% Chocolate Malt → perceptible chocolate note
- 1% Black Malt → sharp colour without much flavour

Dark malts have disproportionate colour and bitterness impact relative to their volume in the recipe.

### Smoked Malts

A special case: kilning over **burning wood or peat** introduces smoke compounds (guaiacol, syringol, phenols) into malt. Viking Smoked Malt uses pear wood smoke. Used in Grodziskie (oak-smoked wheat malt) and Smoked Lager.

Smoke intensity is a function of:
- Wood type (peat > beech > cherry > alder for intensity)
- Smoke contact time
- Peat smoke: medicinal, iodine (Scotch whisky character); wood smoke: gentler, food-smoke quality

### Viking Cookie Malt

A specialty malt engineered via controlled kilning to produce "biscuit, cookie-like" flavours — a specific application of Maillard reaction control. Used in small quantities to add complexity to Pilsner Rosé and other recipes calling for "biscuit-type nuances."

## Contradictions

> **Colour vs flavour**: EBC (colour) is a convenient proxy for roasting intensity but is an imperfect predictor of flavour. Two malts with identical EBC — one roasted barley, one dehusked Carafa Special — will have dramatically different flavour profiles. Carafa Special is dehusked before roasting, eliminating the husk tannins that make roasted barley harsh, even at equivalent colour. Brewers must understand flavour mechanism, not just EBC number.

> **Maillard vs caramelisation in caramel malts**: The handbook describes caramel malts produced by "stewing" green malt (high moisture, high temperature). This environment favours caramelisation (sugar degradation) over Maillard (amino acid + sugar). Some industry texts describe the same process as Maillard-driven. Both reactions likely occur simultaneously, but the dominant one depends on moisture, temperature, and timing — the relative contribution varies across producers and kiln designs.
