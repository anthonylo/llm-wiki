## Summary

Stout is a very dark, top-fermented beer with character determined primarily by roasted malts. With roots tracing to London but more strongly associated with Ireland, stouts represent an evolution of the porter style. Stouts are generally considered stronger than porters, with high original gravity and distinctive roasted flavor profiles.

## Explanation

### Background

Stouts and porters trace their roots back to London, although since then they have been more strongly associated with Ireland. Porters were once the more common beer but were in many cases replaced by stouts. Both are still brewed in the UK, USA, and Baltic countries. Their character is primarily determined by roasted malts.

### Characteristics

Stouts are very dark, top-fermented beers, with color in most cases being near black. Their taste is usually dominated by roasted compounds of dark malts. The original gravity varies depending on the specific stout style. Stouts tend to be dry beers, tasting a little acrid or even harsh. Stout is generally considered stronger than [[porter]].

### Typical Analysis

- **Original gravity**: 13-18 °Plato
- **Alcohol content**: 5-8% volume
- **Color**: 70-200 °EBC
- **Bitterness**: 25-50 BU

### Malt Composition

Well-modified pale brewing malts like Pilsner Malt form a good basis for stout. Very dark color is achieved using relatively high proportions (up to 10%) of:

- Chocolate Malt Light
- Black Malt
- Pearled Black Malt
- Roasted Barley

Other dark malts such as Caramel Malt 150 can also be used to emphasize sweetness or fullness of taste.

### Brewing Notes

When relatively high proportions of dark malts are used, the decreased enzymatic activity of the grist must be taken into consideration. This usually means a longer mashing procedure or supplementation of missing enzymes with highly active malt like Enzyme Malt.

## Contradictions

**Stout vs. Porter**: The handbook notes that stouts are "generally considered stronger than porter," with stout typically ranging 5-8% ABV. However, this is a stylistic distinction rather than a hard rule—individual porters and stouts can have overlapping ABV ranges. The distinction between the two styles has evolved historically, with stouts emerging from and eventually replacing porters in many markets, rather than being fundamentally different categories.
