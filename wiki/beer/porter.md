## Summary

Porter is a very dark, top-fermented beer with historical significance in British brewing traditions. Though once more common than stout, porters have been partially replaced by stouts in many markets. Like stouts, porters are characterized by their reliance on roasted malts for flavor and color, with a near-black appearance and roasted taste profile.

## Explanation

### Background

Porters trace their roots back to London and share a long brewing history with stouts. Once the more common of the two dark beer styles, porters have in many cases been replaced by the stronger [[stout]] style. However, porters continue to be brewed in the UK, USA, and Baltic countries, maintaining their traditional heritage. The character of both porters and stouts is primarily determined by roasted malts.

### Characteristics

Porters are very dark, top-fermented beers, typically near-black in color. Their taste is usually dominated by roasted compounds from dark malts. The original gravity can vary depending on the specific porter style. Like stouts, porters tend to be dry beers with an acrid or slightly harsh character. Generally, porters are considered less strong than stouts, though individual examples may vary.

### Typical Characteristics

While the handbook provides specific analysis for [[stout]] (13-18 °Plato, 5-8% ABV), porter analysis is referenced as being in a similar range but typically lower than stout:

- **Original gravity**: Typically lower than stout
- **Alcohol content**: Typically 4-6% volume (estimated from stout comparison)
- **Color**: Near-black, 70-150 °EBC (typically)
- **Bitterness**: Moderate to high, 20-40 BU (estimated)

### Malt Composition

Similar to [[stout]], porters use well-modified pale brewing malts like Pilsner Malt as a base, with dark color achieved through roasted malts such as:

- Chocolate Malt
- Black Malt
- Roasted Barley

The proportion of dark malts is typically slightly lower than in stout to achieve the less intense flavor profile.

### Brewing Notes

When dark malts are used, the decreased enzymatic activity of the grist must be taken into account. This may require longer mashing procedures or supplementation with highly active malts like Enzyme Malt.

## Contradictions

**Porter vs. Stout**: The handbook indicates that "porters were in many cases replaced by stouts" and that "stout is generally considered stronger than porter." However, the handbook does not provide explicit analytical data for porter (original gravity, ABV, etc.). The distinction is historical and stylistic—porters are the older style, but modern porters and stouts can have overlapping characteristics. This represents an evolution in brewing preferences rather than a fundamental difference.
