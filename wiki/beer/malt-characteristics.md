## Summary

Malt quality is characterised by a standard set of analytical parameters that predict brewing performance. The most important are extract yield (how much fermentable sugar a malt provides), colour (EBC units), diastatic power (enzyme activity), and protein content. Understanding these parameters allows brewers to design recipes with predictable outcomes and troubleshoot process problems.

## Explanation

### Key Parameters

#### Extract (% d.m. — dry matter basis)

The percentage of the malt's dry weight that dissolves into wort as soluble material.
- Higher extract → more fermentable sugars per kg of malt → more economical
- Typical range: 78–83% for base malts; lower for specialty malts
- Measured by the EBC Congress Mash method (65°C, 1 hour)
- **Fine/coarse grind difference** also reported: indicates degree of modification

| Malt | Extract (% d.m.) |
|------|----------------|
| Pilsner Malt | ~81 |
| Vienna Malt | ~80 |
| Munich Light | ~80 |
| Wheat Malt | ~82 |
| Caramel Malt | ~75–78 |
| Roasted products | Very low (starch destroyed) |

---

#### Moisture (%)

Water content of the finished malt:
- Base malts: ~4–5%
- Specialty malts: varies
- Low moisture → better storage stability; prevents mould and premature enzyme degradation
- Moisture above 6% risks quality problems in storage

---

#### Colour (EBC — European Brewing Convention)

Colour is measured by the EBC standard on a congress wort (not the malt itself). EBC units correlate directly to beer colour prediction.

| EBC Range | Malt Type |
|-----------|-----------|
| 3–5 | Pilsner, Pilsner Zero |
| 5–10 | Pale Ale, Vienna |
| 10–25 | Munich Light/Dark |
| 40–250 | Caramel malts |
| 800–1400 | Roasted malts / Roasted barley |

Beer colour is calculated as: `Colour (EBC) ≈ Σ(malt_kg × malt_EBC) / total_wort_litres`

---

#### Diastatic Power (WK — Windisch-Kolbach units)

Measures the total enzymatic activity of the malt — primarily α-amylase and β-amylase — which convert starch to fermentable sugars during mashing.

- **High diastatic power**: base malts (300–400+ WK) — can convert their own starch plus adjuncts
- **Low/zero diastatic power**: caramel and roasted malts — rely on base malt enzymes
- Rule of thumb: the malt bill should average at least 100–150 WK for complete mash conversion
- Important for recipes with large proportions of unmalted adjuncts (wheat, oats, corn, rice)

| Malt | Diastatic Power (WK d.m.) |
|------|--------------------------|
| Pilsner Malt | ~350 |
| Vienna Malt | ~330 |
| Munich Light | ~100 |
| Caramel Malt | ~0 |
| Roasted Products | ~0 |

---

#### Protein (% d.m.)

Total protein content of the malt:
- Typical range: 10–12% for well-modified malts
- **Too high**: haze, harsh astringency, fermentation problems
- **Too low**: poor head retention, insufficient yeast nutrition
- Wheat malt naturally higher protein than barley → contributes to foam

Proteins break down during malting (modification) and mashing (protein rest) into:
- High molecular weight proteins → foam, haze
- Medium molecular weight → haze risk
- Low molecular weight (amino acids / FAN) → yeast nutrition

---

#### FAN (Free Amino Nitrogen, mg/L)

Measures the short-chain amino acids and small peptides available for yeast nutrition:
- Typical range: 100–200 mg/L in congress wort
- Too low → sluggish fermentation, off-flavours, reduced yeast health
- Too high → risk of off-flavours (fusel alcohols) at very high levels
- Caramel malts have lower FAN (proteins more denatured during production)

| Malt | FAN (mg/L) |
|------|-----------|
| Pilsner | ~160 |
| Vienna | ~140 |
| Munich Light | ~110 |
| Caramel Malt | ~65 |

---

#### pH

Congress wort pH:
- Typical base malt range: 5.8–6.1
- Lower pH during mashing (5.2–5.4) is preferred for optimal enzyme activity → typically achieved by water chemistry adjustment (lactic acid, acidulated malt)
- Roasted malts are naturally acidic and lower wort pH

---

#### Viscosity (mPas)

Wort viscosity — affected primarily by beta-glucan content:
- High viscosity → slow wort runoff, filter blockages
- Caused by under-modified malt or high wheat/oat content
- Wheat malt always gives higher viscosity (no husk to filter bed)
- Typical base malt range: 1.4–1.6 mPas

## Contradictions

> **EBC vs SRM**: This handbook uses EBC (European standard). American brewing literature uses SRM (Standard Reference Method). Approximate conversion: EBC ≈ SRM × 2.0 (EBC values are roughly double SRM values). Using handbooks from different regions without converting can lead to significant colour miscalculations.
