## Summary

Lager is the most popular beer type in the world, made possible by the invention of refrigeration systems in the late 19th century. It is a bottom-fermented beer characterized by its pale color, moderate hopping, and highly drinkable nature, with minimal distinctive aroma.

## Explanation

### Background

Lager is the most popular beer type globally, though it is relatively young as a style. The development of refrigeration systems in the late 19th century made it possible to maintain the required low processing temperatures throughout the year, allowing lager production to become widespread. Since then, lager has rapidly overtaken top-fermented beers in terms of volume consumed. The term 'lager' is sometimes used incorrectly to refer to all bottom-fermented beers. Strong lagers are known as exports, while lighter versions are called light beers.

### Characteristics

Lager is a bottom-fermented beer, originally stored for long periods in cold conditions. It is pale in color and moderately hopped, with a flavor not usually characterized by any particular aroma. The style is best described as highly drinkable, with a clean, crisp taste profile.

### Typical Analysis

- **Original gravity**: 10-11.5 °Plato
- **Alcohol content**: 4-5.5% volume
- **Color**: 7-15 °EBC
- **Bitterness**: 15-25 BU

### Malt Composition

Pale Pilsner Malt makes up to 100% of the grain bill. A less or more modified malt should be selected depending on the particular beer characteristics desired (mouth feel, foam, etc.). For flavor enrichment, portions of the grist can be replaced with Caramel Pale Malt or Vienna Malt. Adjuncts can also be used.

### Brewing Notes

No particular brewing precautions are required. However, if a very high proportion of malt with low amylolytic activity is used, this should be taken into account during the mashing procedure. Active base malt is recommended when using such malts.
