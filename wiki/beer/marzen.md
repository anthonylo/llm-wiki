## Summary

Märzen (also known as Vienna Lager or Oktoberfest beer) is a bottom-fermented beer with historical roots in early spring brewing in Germany. The name derives from the German word "märz" (March), referring to the traditional practice of brewing large quantities of strong beer in March for storage and consumption during autumn festivals.

## Explanation

### Background

The name 'Märzen' comes from the German word "märz," meaning March. Before the development of efficient cooling systems, brewing was not always possible during the summer. This led to large quantities of strong beer being brewed in early spring (around March) and stored in cool cellars over the summer. In autumn, these beer kegs were traditionally opened at Oktoberfests. Märzen is also known as Vienna Lager or Oktoberfest beer. Today, Märzens are typical seasonal or festival beers in many countries.

### Characteristics

Märzens are bottom-fermented beers with color ranging from gold to copper or bronze. The taste is very malty and sweet. Hopping is focused on producing a variety of aromas. These beers have a higher original gravity than pilsners or ordinary lagers.

### Typical Analysis

- **Original gravity**: 12-14 °Plato
- **Alcohol content**: 5-6.5% volume
- **Color**: 10-40 °EBC
- **Bitterness**: 20-30 BU

### Malt Composition

Vienna Malt and Munich Light & Dark Malts are special malts developed specifically for these beers. Malt taste can be rounded off using caramel malts or other dark malts to achieve desired flavor profiles.

### Brewing Notes

No particular brewing precautions are required for Märzen production.
