## Summary

Ale is a top-fermented beer style with a wide range of variations in color, flavor, and strength. Characterized by fruity and subtle flavors resulting from higher fermentation temperatures, ales range in color from pale copper to near-black. Post-hopping is common, creating diverse aroma varieties within the ale category.

## Explanation

### Background

Treating ales as a single beer type lacks complete justification since there are many different types and styles within the ale category. However, ales share fundamental characteristics that distinguish them from lagers, primarily their top fermentation process using different yeast strains.

### Characteristics

Ales are fruity and subtle in flavor, mainly due to higher fermentation temperatures. Their color range is wide, starting from pale ales (copper) to bitter ales (amber) and even near-black of some traditional ales. Post-hopping is very common for ales, with aroma varieties created in the brew kettle. Ales are normally dry in taste, though there can be great variation in original gravity depending on the specific style.

### Typical Analysis

- **Original gravity**: 9-13 °Plato
- **Alcohol content**: 3.5-5.5% volume
- **Color**: 15-30 °EBC
- **Bitterness**: 20-30 BU

### Malt Composition

Pale Ale Malt or well-modified Pilsner Malt forms the basis. A stronger golden hue and taste can be achieved from Golden Ale Malt. A wide variety of special malts can be used in accordance with the product type:

- **Bitter Ales**: Cookie Malt
- **Pale Ales**: Pale Caramel Malt
- **Brown Ales**: Dark Caramel Malts

### Brewing Notes

No particular brewing precautions are required for ale production.
