## Summary

Hops (*Humulus lupulus*) are the primary source of bitterness, aroma, and preservation in beer. Alpha acids in hops isomerise during the boil to produce iso-alpha acids — the bitterness compounds. Essential oils from hops contribute floral, citrus, pine, and spicy aromas that survive when hops are added late in the boil or post-fermentation. Hop character is one of the principal differentiators between beer styles.

## Explanation

### Why Hops?

Before widespread hop use (15th–16th century), beer was bittered with "gruit" — blends of herbs. Hops replaced gruit for three reasons:
1. **Bitterness balance** — counteracts malt sweetness
2. **Preservation** — iso-alpha acids and hop oils are antimicrobial
3. **Flavour and aroma** — hundreds of aromatic compounds

### Alpha Acids and Bitterness

The **alpha acid** content of hops (expressed as a percentage) determines bittering potential. During the boil, alpha acids isomerise into **iso-alpha acids** — water-soluble, stable bitterness compounds.

**Isomerisation efficiency** depends on:
- Boil time (longer boil → more isomerisation; diminishing returns after 60 min)
- Boil pH (lower pH reduces efficiency)
- Hop form (pellets > whole cones; higher surface area)

**Bitterness measurement**: International Bitterness Units (IBU) or BU (Bitterness Units as used in Viking Malt handbook). Roughly equivalent at normal brewing conditions.

### Hop Addition Timing

| Addition Point | Boil Time Remaining | Effect |
|---------------|-------------------|--------|
| **Bittering** | 60 min | Maximum isomerisation → high bitterness, minimal aroma (volatiles driven off) |
| **Flavour** | 15–20 min | Partial isomerisation → moderate bitterness + some aroma |
| **Aroma / Late addition** | 5 min or flameout | Minimal isomerisation → maximum aroma retention |
| **Whirlpool** | 0 min (flameout) | No isomerisation; maximum essential oil retention; used in many Viking recipes |
| **Dry hopping** | Post-fermentation (cold) | Zero isomerisation, zero bitterness; purely aroma compounds from cold extraction |

### Hop Varieties Used in Viking Recipes

| Hop | Origin | Character | Used In |
|-----|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Saaz** | Czech | Noble; earthy, spicy, herbal; very low alpha (~3–4%) | Pilsner (bittering + aroma), Dark Red Lager |
| **Cascade** | USA | Citrus (grapefruit), floral; medium alpha (~5–7%) | Viking Red Lager, Citric Wheat Beer, Smoked Lager |
| **IKE Nugget** | High alpha | Clean bittering, neutral aroma | Gluten-Free Lager (bittering) |
| **Magnum** | German | Clean high-alpha bittering; low aroma; ~13% | Alcohol-Free Lager |
| **New World varieties** | USA/NZ | Citrus, tropical, pine, floral; popular in craft | American IPA, NEIPA, APA |

### Bitterness by Style (from Viking recipes)

| Beer | IBU/BU | Character |
|------|--------|-----------|
| Basic Lager | Low (~18–22) | Subtle, balancing |
| Pilsner Rosé | ~25–30 | Noticeable but not aggressive |
| Viking CaraBody Pils | 28 BU | Crisp, clean |
| Dark Red Lager | 26 BU | Moderate, balancing richness |
| Porter | 34 BU | Higher for roasted malt balance |
| American IPA | 50–70 BU | High hop bitterness |
| Baltic Porter | Low–medium | Malt-dominant; hops subordinate |

### Essential Oils and Aroma

Hop aroma comes from **essential oils** — myrcene, linalool, geraniol, humulene, caryophyllene. These are volatile and largely lost during extended boiling.

Key aroma descriptors:
- **Myrcene**: citrus, fresh, resinous
- **Linalool**: floral, lavender
- **Geraniol**: rose, floral, sweet
- **Humulene**: earthy, woody, noble (dominant in Saaz, Hallertau)
- **Caryophyllene**: spicy, pepper

### Dry Hopping

Adding hops post-fermentation extracts aromatic oils without bitterness. Key to **NEIPA** (New England IPA) style — producing "juicy," tropical aroma without harsh bitterness. Viking's Session DDH Hazy IPA uses double dry hopping (DDH) with citrusy hop varieties.

### Hop Products

| Form | Description | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------|
| **Whole cone** | Dried natural hop flower | Requires more storage space; some brewers prefer flavour |
| **Pellet (T90)** | Hammer-milled and compressed | Standard form; more consistent alpha; better shelf life |
| **T45/Cryo hops** | Concentrated lupulin fraction | High aroma intensity; used in dry hopping |
| **Hop extract** | CO₂ or ethanol-extracted alpha acids | Precise bittering; reduced dry hopping complexity |

### The Juniper Exception (Viking Handbook)

The Viking Single Malt Juniper Lager uses **Juniper Chips** added during whirlpool — not a hop at all, but a botanical additive providing similar bittering/aromatic purpose. This reflects the pre-hop tradition of botanical bittering agents.

## Contradictions

> **BU vs IBU**: The Viking handbook uses "BU" (Bitterness Units); most international brewing literature uses "IBU" (International Bitterness Units). They measure the same thing (iso-alpha acids in mg/L) and are numerically equivalent at typical levels, but the notation differs by convention.

> **Bitterness perception vs measurement**: IBU measures chemical iso-alpha acid concentration, not perceived bitterness. A 60 IBU stout with dense roasted malt may taste less bitter than a 40 IBU NEIPA, because residual sweetness and body suppress bitterness perception. Some brewers argue IBU is a poor proxy for consumer experience.
