## Summary

Caramel malts (also called crystal malts) are specialty malts made by stewing green malt at high moisture before kilning, causing sugars to caramelise inside the grain. The result is a glassy, partially non-fermentable endosperm that contributes sweetness, body, red-to-brown colour, and caramel/toffee flavours to beer — without requiring mashing enzymes.

## Explanation

### Production Method

Caramel malts are unique because the starch conversion (saccharification) happens **inside the grain** during production:

1. Green malt (germinated, not yet kilned) is loaded wet into the kiln
2. Stewing at 60–70°C (high moisture) converts starch to sugars in situ
3. Temperature rises: sugars caramelise inside the sealed endosperm
4. Grain is dried/kilned to lock the caramelised structure in place

Result: the endosperm is glassy (crystalline sugar) rather than floury starch. These **crystallised sugars are largely non-fermentable** — they pass through fermentation intact, contributing residual sweetness and body.

### Colour Range and Flavour Spectrum

| Caramel Malt | EBC Colour | Flavour Profile | Use |
|-------------|-----------|-----------------|-----|
| Light (Viking Caramel 10) | ~8–12 EBC | Honey, light caramel, soft sweetness | Pale ales, light lagers |
| Medium (Viking Caramel 50) | ~40–60 EBC | Toffee, rich caramel, amber | Amber ales, red ales, Märzen |
| Dark (Viking Caramel 200) | ~180–220 EBC | Dark fruit, raisin, plum, molasses | Stouts, strong ales, dark amber |

### Key Properties

- **Zero diastatic power** — cannot self-convert; must be used with base malt
- **Pre-converted** — dissolves directly in hot water (no mashing required, though mashing still happens)
- **Body enhancement** — non-fermentable dextrins and caramelised sugars increase viscosity and mouthfeel
- **Colour stability** — contributes red-amber-dark colours that are lightfast
- **Head retention** — proteins assist foam stability

### Typical Usage Rates

| Application | Caramel Malt % |
|------------|---------------|
| Pale ales (light character) | 5–10% |
| Red ales / amber lagers | 10–20% |
| Dark ales / stout | 5–15% (dark caramel) |
| Body/sweetness without colour | 5–10% (light caramel) |

## Contradictions

> **Maillard vs caramelisation**: Some texts describe caramel malt flavours as Maillard-driven (amino acid + sugar at heat). The Viking handbook's production description (high moisture stewing → sugar crystallisation → caramelisation) suggests caramelisation dominates. In practice, both reactions occur simultaneously; the relative contribution varies with kiln design and timing.
