## Summary

Chocolate Malt is a roasted specialty malt kilned at 180–200°C to develop deep brown colour (~800 EBC) and intense chocolate, coffee, and bittersweet notes. Despite its name it contains no actual chocolate — the flavour comes entirely from intense Maillard reaction and pyrolysis products. It is used in small quantities (3–8%) to define stouts, porters, and dark ales.

## Explanation

### Characteristics

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Colour | ~700–1000 EBC |
| Flavour | Milk chocolate, bittersweet chocolate, coffee, slight nuttiness |
| Bitterness | Moderate — less harsh than black malt or roasted barley |
| Diastatic power | Zero — fully enzyme-destroyed during roasting |
| Extract yield | Reduced vs pale malt |

### How It Differs from Other Dark Malts

| Malt | EBC | Flavour | Harshness |
|------|-----|---------|-----------|
| Chocolate Malt | ~800 | Chocolate, coffee, mild bitter | Moderate |
| Black Malt | ~1200–1400 | Dry espresso, char, very bitter | High |
| Roasted Barley | ~1000–1300 | Dry bitter coffee, roast | High (husk tannins) |
| Carafa Special III | ~1400 | Coffee, chocolate — dehusked | Low (no husk tannins) |
| Caramel 200 | ~200 | Dark fruit, raisin, molasses | Very low |

The key distinction: **Chocolate Malt** produces smooth, rounded chocolate and coffee character. **Black Malt** and **Roasted Barley** push further into harsh, dry, astringent territory. The husk of roasted barley is a major source of drying tannins — dehusked variants (Carafa Special) reduce harshness while maintaining colour.

### Flavour Chemistry

Kilning at 180–200°C drives:
- **Intense Maillard reaction**: melanoidins, hundreds of volatile aromatics
- **Early pyrolysis**: partial thermal degradation of sugars and amino acids
- **Furfuryl compounds**: characteristic chocolate/coffee aromatic precursors

### Typical Usage

| Beer Style | Chocolate Malt % | Effect |
|-----------|-----------------|--------|
| Porter | 5–10% | Core chocolate/coffee character |
| Sweet Stout | 5–8% | Chocolate note balanced by lactose sweetness |
| Dry Stout | 5–8% | Plus roasted barley for dry finish |
| Brown Ale | 2–5% | Background chocolate complexity |
| Dark Lager | 1–3% | Subtle colour + roast note |

### Rule of Thumb

5% Chocolate Malt in an otherwise pale recipe = clearly perceptible chocolate-coffee note and dark colour.
