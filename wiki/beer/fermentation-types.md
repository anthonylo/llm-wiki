## Summary

Beer fermentation is classified by yeast behaviour and temperature: **top fermentation** (ales — warm, 15–25°C, yeast rises to surface) and **bottom fermentation** (lagers — cold, 4–12°C, yeast settles at the bottom). This distinction has profound implications for flavour profile, brewing process, and infrastructure requirements. Bottom fermentation only became viable globally after refrigeration was invented in the late 19th century.

## Explanation

### Top Fermentation (Ales)

- **Yeast species**: *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* (ale strains)
- **Temperature**: 15–25°C (ambient or slightly warm)
- **Yeast behaviour**: rises to the surface during active fermentation; forms a yeast head
- **Fermentation duration**: typically 5–10 days for primary fermentation
- **Conditioning**: shorter cold conditioning period (1–4 weeks)

**Flavour consequences**:
- Warm fermentation produces more esters (fruity: banana, apple, pear, stone fruit)
- Belgian strains produce phenols (clove, pepper, spice)
- More complex, varied flavour profiles
- Generally fuller body

**Yeast examples**:
- Safale US-05 (American ale yeast): clean, neutral ester profile → used for APA, IPA
- Belgian strains: high ester and phenol production → Dubbel, Tripel, Belgian IPA
- Hefeweizen strains: produces isoamyl acetate (banana) and 4-vinyl guaiacol (clove)

### Bottom Fermentation (Lagers)

- **Yeast species**: *Saccharomyces pastorianus* (lager strains; a hybrid of *S. cerevisiae* × *S. eubayanus*)
- **Temperature**: 4–12°C (requires active cooling or cold climate)
- **Yeast behaviour**: settles to the bottom at end of fermentation
- **Fermentation duration**: longer primary (10–14+ days) + extended cold conditioning (lagering)
- **Lagering**: cold storage at near-freezing temperatures for weeks to months; clarifies beer, smooths flavours

**Flavour consequences**:
- Cold fermentation suppresses ester formation → clean, crisp character
- Neutral flavour allows malt and hop character to shine through
- Smooth, refreshing, fewer fruity notes

**Yeast examples** (from Viking recipes):
- Saflager W-34/70: classic German lager yeast; clean fermentation; 12°C
- Saflager S-23: slightly more estery; higher attenuation
- Lallemand Diamond Lager: clean, cold-tolerant

**Historical note**: Lager's dominance (>50% of global beer consumption) was made possible by the invention of mechanical refrigeration in the late 19th century. Before that, lagers could only be brewed in winter or in cold cellars (as Märzen was).

### Typical Fermentation Schedules (from Viking Recipes)

**Ale (e.g., Viking APA)**:
- 14 days at 19°C primary fermentation
- 2 days at 5°C cold crash
- Total: ~16 days

**Lager (e.g., Viking Premium Lager)**:
- 14 days at 12°C primary fermentation
- Extended cold conditioning at near-freezing

**Lager (Viking Low Lager)**:
- 5 days at +13°C
- 2 days at +15°C (diacetyl rest)
- 5 days at +5°C
- 2 days at -1°C (cold conditioning)
- 0.15 bar counter-pressure
- Total: ~14 days

### Key Fermentation Parameters

| Parameter | Ale | Lager |
|-----------|-----|-------|
| Yeast type | *S. cerevisiae* | *S. pastorianus* |
| Primary temp | 15–25°C | 4–12°C |
| Duration | 5–10 days | 10–14+ days |
| Ester production | High | Low |
| Phenol production | Strain-dependent | Very low |
| Conditioning | Short (1–4 weeks) | Long (2–12 weeks) |
| Clarity | Often hazy (esp. NEIPA, Hefe) | Naturally clear after lagering |

### Hybrid Styles

Some beers blend fermentation approaches:
- **Baltic Porter**: traditionally a porter/stout profile (dark, roasted) brewed with lager yeast at cold temperatures → smooth, clean dark beer with high alcohol
- **Steam Beer (California Common)**: uses lager yeast at ale temperatures → clean but slightly more estery than typical lager

### Diacetyl Rest

In lager brewing, a brief temperature rise (12°C → 15°C) near the end of primary fermentation allows the yeast to reabsorb diacetyl (a buttery off-flavour precursor). The Viking Low Lager recipe explicitly includes this step (2 days at +15°C). Missing this step results in butterscotch-flavoured beer.
