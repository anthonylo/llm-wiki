## Summary

Pale malt is the foundational base malt of most beers — a lightly kilned barley malt that provides fermentable extract, diastatic power (enzymes), and a clean, neutral background flavour. The term covers several closely related varieties that differ mainly in kilning temperature and the resulting flavour intensity.

## Explanation

### What Is Pale Malt?

Pale malt is barley that has been steeped, germinated, and kilned at low temperatures (80–90°C) to preserve enzymatic activity while developing mild Maillard flavours. The result is a cream-to-gold coloured malt with:

- **Colour**: 3–5 EBC (very pale)
- **Extract yield**: ~80% dry basis (high — this is its primary job)
- **Diastatic power**: 250–350 WK (high — sufficient to convert its own starch plus adjuncts)
- **Flavour**: Bready, fresh, lightly grainy — serves as a neutral backdrop

### Main Pale Malt Varieties

| Malt | Colour (EBC) | Flavour | Typical Use |
|------|-------------|---------|-------------|
| **Pilsner Malt** | 2–4 | Very light, neutral, slightly sweet | Pilsner, pale lager, light lager |
| **Pale Ale Malt** | 4–7 | Biscuity, slightly more malt character | Pale ales, bitters, IPAs |
| **Vienna Malt** | 7–10 | Honey, light biscuit, bread crust | Vienna lager, Märzen, amber lager |
| **Munich Malt** | 14–20 | Rich malt, honey, amber | Märzen, Bock, malt-forward ales |

### Role in a Recipe

Base malts typically constitute 60–90% of the grain bill. They:
- Provide the bulk of fermentable sugars (glucose, maltose, maltotriose)
- Supply enzymes (α- and β-amylase) to convert starch in the mash
- Set the baseline colour and flavour for the beer

Specialty malts (caramel, roasted) are used in small percentages on top of a pale malt base.
