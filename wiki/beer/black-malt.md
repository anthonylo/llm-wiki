## Summary

Black Malt (also called Black Patent Malt) is the most intensely roasted malted barley, kilned at 200–220°C+ to produce extreme dark colour (1200–1500 EBC) and harsh, dry, charred flavours. It is used primarily as a **colour agent** in small quantities (0.5–2%), where its bitterness contribution must be carefully managed.

## Explanation

### Characteristics

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Colour | 1200–1500 EBC |
| Diastatic power | Zero |
| Flavour | Dry espresso, charred, tar, very bitter, astringent |
| Harshness | Very high at >2% |
| Primary use | Colour, roast bitterness backbone |

### Black Malt vs Roasted Barley vs Chocolate Malt

| Malt | EBC | Flavour | Harshness | Best Use |
|------|-----|---------|-----------|---------|
| Chocolate Malt | ~800 | Smooth chocolate, coffee | Moderate | Balanced dark beers |
| Roasted Barley | ~1000–1300 | Dry bitter coffee | High | Dry Stout |
| Black Malt | ~1200–1500 | Char, tar, very bitter | Very high | Colour + extreme dry stout |
| Carafa Special III | ~1400 | Coffee, chocolate (dehusked) | Low | Smooth dark lager |

### Usage Guidelines

Black malt has the greatest colour-per-gram of any malt:
- **0.5%**: Noticeable colour shift; almost no flavour perception
- **1%**: Sharp colour darkening; faint roast hint
- **2%**: Clearly perceptible dry, bitter character; deep colour
- **>3%**: Harsh, astringent, tarry — usually undesirable except in strong robust styles

### When to Use Black Malt

Black malt is chosen when:
1. **Maximum colour without sweetness** — adding chocolate malt to hit very dark colours also adds chocolate sweetness; black malt adds colour more efficiently
2. **Dry, harsh finish is desired** — robust porters, American stouts
3. **Colour correction in blending** — adding 0.5–1% to hit a target EBC

For smooth dark beers (Schwarzbier, cream stout), dehusked alternatives (Carafa Special III) are preferred because they achieve comparable colour without the harsh husk tannins.

### Viking Product

Viking's specialty malt range includes high-colour dark malts equivalent to black malt for colour and roast character. The Viking Dark Red Lager recipe uses small additions of high-EBC roasted malt to achieve amber-red colour.
