## Summary

The brewing process transforms malted grain, water, hops, and yeast into beer through a sequence of controlled steps: milling, mashing (converting starch to sugar), wort separation, boiling (sterilisation and hop isomerisation), fermentation (yeast converts sugars to alcohol), conditioning, and filtration. Each step has measurable parameters that the brewer controls to achieve a target beer style.

## Explanation

### Overview of Steps

```
MALT → MILL → MASH → LAUTER (separate wort) → BOIL + HOPS
→ WHIRLPOOL → COOL → FERMENT → CONDITION → FILTER → PACKAGE
```

---

### Step 1: Milling

Malt is crushed to:
- Break open the husk while keeping it largely intact (lautering filter bed)
- Expose starchy endosperm to water
- Avoid over-grinding to flour (filtration problems, astringency)

---

### Step 2: Mashing

Crushed malt mixed with hot water at controlled temperatures. Enzymes active during malting now convert starch to sugars.

**Key temperature rests**:

| Rest | Temperature | Purpose |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Acid rest | 35–40°C | Lower pH via phytase activity |
| Beta-glucan rest | 40–45°C | Reduce viscosity (important for wheat/oat) |
| Protein rest | 50–55°C | Break down proteins (FAN production, head, haze) |
| Beta-amylase rest | 60–65°C | Produce fermentable maltose (dry beer) |
| Alpha-amylase rest | 70–72°C | Produce non-fermentable dextrins (fuller body) |
| Mash-out | 78°C | Denature enzymes; arrest conversion |

**Example mash programme** (Viking Low Lager):
- Malt:Water ratio 1:7.5
- pH settles at 5.3–5.5 (no adjustment needed)
- 63°C → ramp up → mash-out

**Single infusion mash**: Most ales and modern well-modified lager malts need only a single temperature rest (63–67°C) — simplified, energy-efficient.

**Multi-step mash**: Used for poorly-modified malts, wheat beers (high beta-glucan), or traditional lager brewing.

---

### Step 3: Lautering (Wort Separation)

The sugary liquid (wort) is separated from the spent grain:
- **Mash tun / lauter tun**: wort runs off through the grain bed (acts as a natural filter)
- **Sparge**: hot water (78°C) rinsed through the grain to extract remaining sugars
- Wheat and oat malts are problematic — no husk → sticky filter bed → may require rice hulls as filter aid

---

### Step 4: Boiling

Wort boiled for 60 minutes (standard). Achieves:
- **Sterilisation**: kills microorganisms
- **Hop isomerisation**: converts alpha acids to iso-alpha acids (bitterness)
- **Protein coagulation**: removes proteins that would cause haze (hot break)
- **DMS reduction**: dimethyl sulphide (cooked corn off-flavour) driven off
- **Concentration**: reduces wort to target pre-boil gravity

**Hop addition timing**:
- **Bittering hops**: beginning of boil (60 min) — maximum isomerisation, aroma driven off
- **Flavour hops**: 15–20 min remaining — partial isomerisation, some aroma retained
- **Aroma hops (whirlpool)**: at flameout / whirlpool — minimal isomerisation, maximum aroma
- **Dry hops**: post-fermentation, cold — aroma only, no bitterness

**Special additions during boiling** (from Viking recipes):
- Juniper Chips (whirlpool): Viking Single Malt Juniper Lager
- Peeled bitter orange + coriander seeds: Citric Wheat Beer
- Lactic acid for pH adjustment

---

### Step 5: Whirlpool and Cooling

After boiling:
- **Whirlpool**: tangential flow creates a vortex; trub (hop and protein debris) collects in a cone in the centre
- **Cooling**: plate heat exchanger rapidly cools wort to fermentation temperature; crucial to avoid DMS formation

---

### Step 6: Fermentation

See [[fermentation-types]] for detailed ale vs. lager fermentation.

Key parameters tracked:
- **OG** (Original Gravity): measured at start; predicts final ABV
- **FG** (Final Gravity): measured at end; determines actual attenuation
- Temperature profile: per yeast strain requirements
- Duration: 5–14 days primary

**Approximate ABV calculation**:
```
ABV ≈ (OG − FG) × 131
(where OG and FG in specific gravity points)
Example: OG=1.048, FG=1.012 → ABV ≈ (48−12) × 0.131 ≈ 4.7%
```

---

### Step 7: Conditioning

After primary fermentation:
- **Ales**: cold crash (2–4°C) for 1–2 days → yeast and dry hop debris settle
- **Lagers**: extended lagering at near-freezing (weeks to months) → natural clarification, smooth flavour
- Diacetyl rest: temperature blip at 15°C before cold crash to reabsorb buttery off-flavours

---

### Step 8: Filtration

For clear beers:
- Sheet filtration (e.g., BECO SD 30, 0.5 μm) — removes yeast and haze particles
- Hazy/unfiltered beers (NEIPA, Hefeweizen): skip filtration intentionally

## Contradictions

> **Single infusion vs multi-step mashing**: Industry practice has shifted toward single-infusion mashing for well-modified malts, but traditional lager and German wheat beer recipes specify multi-step programmes. The Viking handbook's recipes use simple mash schedules (single or dual rest) reflecting modern well-modified malts. Traditional multi-step programmes add complexity without consistent benefit for modern malt.
