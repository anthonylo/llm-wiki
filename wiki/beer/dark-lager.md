## Summary

Dark Lager is a bottom-fermented beer with historical significance predating pale lagers. Strongly associated with central European brewing traditions, particularly Bavaria, dark lagers feature color ranging from amber to near-black with flavor dominated by dark malt compounds such as caramel, chocolate, and toffee.

## Explanation

### Background

Before pale bottom-fermented beers became popular, almost all beers were dark in color. Dark lagers were strongly associated with brewing traditions in different areas of central Europe, particularly in Bavaria and other regions. These beers represent an older brewing tradition that preceded the modern pale lager era.

### Characteristics

The color of dark lagers varies but is usually something between amber and near-black. The aroma tends to be dominated by compounds from dark malts: caramel, chocolate, toffee, etc. Bitterness is not emphasized in dark lagers; the taste is usually sweet and full-bodied. Dark lagers are normally not very strong, rarely exceeding 6% alcohol by volume.

### Typical Analysis

- **Original gravity**: 10-15 °Plato
- **Alcohol content**: 3.5-6% volume
- **Color**: 50-150 °EBC
- **Bitterness**: 15-30 BU

### Malt Composition

Many kinds of special malt can be used in dark lager production, selected to achieve the desired dark color and malt-forward character. The specific malt bill depends on the target flavor profile and darkness level.

### Brewing Notes

No particular brewing precautions are required for dark lager production.
