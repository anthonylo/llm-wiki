## Summary

Roasted malts are specialty malts or grains roasted at very high temperatures (180–220°C+) without kilning first, producing extreme colour (900–1500+ EBC) and intensely bitter, dry, coffee-and-char flavours. The two primary roasted malts are **roasted barley** (unmalted) and **black malt** (malted then roasted), both used sparingly to define dry stout, porter, and very dark beer styles.

## Explanation

### Roasted Malt vs Kilned Dark Malt

The key distinction:

| | **Kilned Dark Malt** (Chocolate Malt) | **Roasted Malt** (Roasted Barley / Black Malt) |
|---|---|---|
| Starting material | Malted barley | Malted barley (black malt) or raw barley (roasted barley) |
| Temperature | 180–200°C (kilning) | 200–220°C+ (roasting drum) |
| Process | Gradual temperature ramp | Rapid high-heat drum roasting |
| Flavour | Chocolate, coffee — rounded | Dry, bitter, espresso, char, tar |
| Harshness | Moderate | High (especially with husk) |

### Primary Roasted Malts

**Roasted Barley**
- Made from *unmalted* raw barley — no germination step
- Colour: 900–1300 EBC
- Flavour: Dry, bitter, dark espresso, astringent (husk tannins present)
- Defines Irish Dry Stout (Guinness-style): ~5–8% of grist
- Imparts characteristic dry finish and dark colour without sweetness

**Black Malt (Black Patent)**
- Made from fully malted barley, then extreme roasting
- Colour: 1200–1500 EBC
- Flavour: Even drier than roasted barley, char, very harsh at >2%
- Used primarily as colour agent; 1% = substantial darkening; 2%+ = perceivably harsh
- Common in robust porters, American stouts

**Carafa Special (Dehusked Black Malt)**
- Black malt with husk removed before roasting
- Colour: ~1000–1400 EBC — comparable to standard black malt
- Flavour: Coffee, dark chocolate — **without harsh tannins**
- Preferred in German Schwarzbier (black lager) where smooth dark character is essential
- Viking equivalent: Viking's dark specialty malts in the specialty range

### Why Harshness Matters

Roasted barley and black malt both contain intact husks during roasting. At 200°C+:
- Husk polyphenols polymerise into harsh, drying tannins
- These contribute the characteristic "dry" finish of Dry Stout
- In styles that require smoothness, they are an off-flavour

The husk is the most important variable controlling whether dark malt is "pleasantly dry" or "harshly astringent."

### Typical Usage

| Beer Style | Roasted Malt | % | Effect |
|-----------|-------------|---|--------|
| Dry Stout | Roasted Barley | 5–8% | Defines style: dry, bitter, dark |
| Robust Porter | Black Malt | 3–5% | Deep colour + roast |
| American Stout | Black Malt | 3–6% | Colour + roast bitterness |
| Schwarzbier | Dehusked Black Malt | 3–6% | Dark colour, smooth finish |
| Dark Lager | Roasted Malt | 1–2% | Colour adjustment, minimal flavour |
