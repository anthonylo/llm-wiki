## Summary

Vienna Malt is a base malt kilned at ~95°C to produce a warm amber colour (7–10 EBC) and a light honey-biscuit flavour. It is the defining malt of Vienna Lager and Märzen, and sits between Pilsner malt (pale, neutral) and Munich malt (darker, richer) on the flavour spectrum.

## Explanation

### Characteristics

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Colour | 7–10 EBC |
| Extract yield | ~78–80% dry basis |
| Diastatic power | 150–250 WK |
| Protein | 10–11% |
| Flavour | Light honey, soft biscuit, slightly toasty |

Vienna malt is a **base malt** — it fully self-converts and provides enough diastatic power to convert small amounts of zero-enzyme specialty malts.

### Historical Context

Vienna malt was developed in Vienna in the 1840s by Anton Dreher, who used indirect kiln heating to produce a pale amber malt that enabled the first amber lager. Before this innovation, most European beer was dark (kilned over direct fire). Vienna malt pioneered modern amber lager styles.

### Flavour Chemistry

Kilned at ~95°C — higher than Pilsner (80°C) but lower than Munich (105°C):
- Light Maillard reactions develop honey and fresh bread notes
- More colour than Pilsner malt (amber-gold vs straw)
- Less intensity than Munich — the "medium" base malt

### Typical Use in Recipes

| Beer Style | Vienna Malt % | Role |
|-----------|--------------|------|
| Vienna Lager | 80–100% | Defines the style |
| Märzen | 30–50% | Combines with Munich for amber character |
| Amber Lager | 40–70% | Amber colour and malt character |
| California Common | 50–80% | Traditional American amber lager base |
| Some Pale Ales | 10–20% | Adds malt complexity without darkening much |

### Vienna Malt vs Neighbouring Malts

| Feature | Pale/Pilsner | Vienna Malt | Munich Malt |
|---------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| EBC | 2–4 | 7–10 | 14–20 |
| Flavour | Neutral, clean | Honey, biscuit | Rich honey, bread crust |
| Best use | Pilsner, light lager | Vienna Lager, Märzen | Märzen, Bock, Dunkel |
